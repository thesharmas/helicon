The thesis

Over two years I took my own company from AI-written marketing emails to an AI-native lender — agents underwriting credit, monitoring the portfolio, and shipping to customers. The lesson from every era was the same: the models kept getting smarter; the real work was wiring them into the business.

Capability was never the bottleneck. Reliability is. The companies winning with AI aren't the ones with the cleverest prompts — they're the ones that built the harness: the tool layer, the evals, the guardrails, the governance that let AI touch real work safely.

That's what I build. And because I've done it end to end at my own company — strategy through architecture through code through production — I don't hand you a deck and a list of implementation partners. I ship it with you.

The discipline

De-LLM-ing: the unfashionable secret

The most valuable move is often removing the LLM: prototype with agents, then compile the workflow down to deterministic code. Faster, cheaper, auditable — and it never hallucinates. Use the LLM to build the automation, then get it out of the loop.

How I work

The unglamorous things that make AI stick

Evals before scale

If you can't measure it, you can't ship it. Every workflow gets an eval harness before it gets a wider rollout.

Ship behind flags

Working software in weeks, gated and reversible. No big-bang launches, no burned trust.

Governance in code

Policy that lives in documents gets skipped. Policy that lives in code gets enforced — identically, every time.

Right-size the intelligence

LLMs where the job is fuzzy, deterministic code where it isn't. The best agent is often the one you retire.

Build the tool layer once

Wire your systems into one governed tool layer (MCP), then swap models and harnesses freely as the frontier moves.

Knowledge compounds

Every engagement leaves behind skills, docs, and evals your team owns — so the system keeps getting better after I leave.
